binomial random variables

requirements for a variable to have a binomial dist.

  • binary: success or failure
  • independent: events don't effect each other
  • fixed # of trials
  • some chance of success for each trial

all have to be satisfied for it to be a binomial dist.

binomial formula

\[P(X=k)=\binom{n}{k} \cdot p^k \cdot (1-p)^{n-k}\]
  • n = # of trials
  • k = # of successes
  • p = chance of success for a single trial
  • 1-p = chance of failure for a single trial
  • n-k = # of failures

attributes & interpretations

\[\mu_x=np\] "after a shitton of trials, the average # of [successes] is \(\mu_x\) out of n"

\[\sigma_x=\sqrt{np(1-p)}\] "the # of [successes] typically varies by \(\sigma_x\) from the mean of \(\mu_x\) out of n"

the geometric distribution

requirements

  • Binary: same as the B in the binomial
  • Independent: same as the I in the binomial
  • Trials until success: yeah, just make sure that's what a var is measuring
  • Same probability of success: same as the S in binomial

attributes

  • geometric formula: \(P(X=x)=(1-p)^{x-1}p\)
  • shape: right-skewed
  • center: \(\mu_x=\frac{1}{p}\)
  • variability: \(\sigma_x=\frac{\sqrt{1-p}}{p}\)
