argument principle

consider an $f$ meromorphic on some domain $\Omega \supseteq \overline{\mathbb{D}}$, where $\mathbb{D}$ is the unit disc.

assuming that there's no pole or zero on the boundary of the disc,

\[\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{|z|=1} \frac{f'(z)}{f(z)}\,dz=Z-P\]

where $Z$ and $P$ are the number of zeros and on the disc respectively, counted with multiplicities

the proof first enumerates all the zeros and poles of $f$ like $z_1, z_2, \cdots, z_Z$ and $p_1, p_2, \cdots, p_P$, with those of order greater than $1$ appearing multiple times

given this, we can write

\[f(z)=\frac{\prod_{j=1}^Z (z-z_j)}{\prod_{k=1}^P (z-p_k)} g(z)\]

where $g$ has no pole or zero in $\overline{\mathbb{D}}$

then, using the fact that $\frac{(fg)'}{fg}=\frac{f'}{f}+\frac{g'}{g}$ (proved by calc bashing),

\[\frac{f'(z)}{f(z)}=\sum_{j=1}^Z \frac{1}{z-z_j} - \sum_{k=1}^P \frac{1}{z-p_k} + \frac{g'(z)}{g(z)}\]

we can use cauchy's integral theorem (i think)? to see that of the $\frac{1}{z-C}$s integrates to exactly $1$, and since $g$ is nonzero $g'/g$ is holomorphic on $\overline{\mathbb{D}}$ and integrates to $0$.

putting all this together should prove that the integral is $Z-P$.

open mapping theorem

yeah apparently this means non-constant holomorphic functions are open mappings, or in other words they map open sets to open sets

so if we have an $a$ and a radius $r$ s.t. $B_r(a) \subseteq \Omega$, we need to show that $f(a)$ has its own ball too in $f(\Omega)$, it's image

first take some $r_1 < r$ s.t. $|z-a|=r_1 \implies f(r_1) \ne f(a)$. we can do this because if we couldn't, that would imply a convergent sequence of distinct points that all map to $f(a)$, making $f$ constant

now this ring around $a$ is compact since it's closed and bounded, and so its image has a positive distance from $a$, and $\inf_{|z-a|=r_1} |f(z)-f(a)|=\epsilon > 0$

i propose that taking $r_2=\frac{\epsilon}{2}$ should work. or just anything that's strictly less than $\epsilon$, tbh, i'm just not sure about making it equal to $\epsilon$ exactly.

but yeah, first notice that we can use the argument principle to say that

\[\int_{|z-a|=r_1} \frac{f'(z)}{f(z)-f(a)}\,dz \ge 1\]

where we're really integrating to find the zeros of $g(z)=f(z)-f(a)$. this one is guaranteed to have a solution (at $a$) and there's no poles inside the circle, so the result should be at least $1$.

another thing to take note is that this integral is continuous with regards to what we put inside for $f(a)$, as long as it doesn't make $f(z)-f(a)$ zero on the ring

now for any $t \in B_{r_2}(f(a))$, $|f(z)-t| > 0$ when $|z-a|=r_1$ by how we chose $r_2$, so we can again integrate and use continuity to see that

\[\int_{|z-a|=r_1} \frac{f'(z)}{f(z)-t}\,dz \ge 1\]

so yeah, $f(z)-t$ has a zero in $B_r(a)$, which means $t$ has a nonempty preimage in $\Omega$ and that $f$ itself maps open sets to open sets
