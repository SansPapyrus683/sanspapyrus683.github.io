peak books

wiseguy

nicholas pileggi

This was the first book of Pileggi's that I read. I watched Goodfellas a couple years ago and actually didn't care much for the characters, but I remembered one day that it was based on this book and placed a hold on it. I think this might've been the first book where I immediately knew it was peak just by reading the first couple of sentences.

Pileggi does this thing where he uses a metric f-ton of dialogue to tell his stories, so at a certain point it feels like a first-person book. It's pulled off very well, even though all the mafia terminology got a bit confusing for a dumba** like me sometimes.

casino: love & honor in las vegas

also nicholas pileggi

Unlike with Goodfellas, I didn't watch Casino before reading this one. I remember hearind somewhere that the movie ran upwards of three hours long and that was just a massive turn off for me.

Told in much the same way as Wiseguy, this book follows the rise and fall of the mafia in Vegas (at least I think so), and does a bit of parallel storytelling with both Frank Rosenthal and Tony Spilotro. I'm realizing as I write this that once you've read one you've basically read the other, but hey! Two cakes, amirite?

blye, private eye

yes, i read all three of this guy pileggi's books

It's really unfortunate he only wrote these three. I probably could've devoured at least like five more of this guy's books.

This book follows a private detective and uses a bit more third person than the other two books. It's still peak regardless. He gives a ton of mini storylines throughout the book that are all neatly tied up at the end, even though according to Pileggi rarely any of the cases PIs are involved in have neat endings.

killers of the flower moon

david grann

I somewhat wanted to watch the movie, but I didn't really have time and saw this in a Shanghai bookstore I was browsing one day and so decided to read it on a whim. Boy was I glad.

This was actually the book that got me to google more nonfiction books that were written like fiction. There's a good amount of photos throughout, and I found myself silently rooting for the investigators as the book wound on.

devil in the white city

erik larson

This book is really two in one. Half the chapters tell the building of the Chicago World's Fair, while the other half tell the story of H. H. Holmes, one of the 19th century's most notorious serial killers.

Larson does this thing where he won't reveal the name of an entity until later in the book for spoiler reasons, and I think it's a pretty cool thing to do in a nonfiction book. The foreshadowing does get a bit too obvious, sometimes. I predicted one of the major plot points like a whole half of the book before it actually happened, and I spent way too long just waiting for it to get to the actual point.

Also some Reddit thread says that half the H. H. Holmes story is, like, fabricated, so read this at your own risk? I guess? Nevertheless, this book is still peak.

the radium girls

kate moore

The book starts out okay. There's a bit too many characters that are introduced, seemingly willy-nilly. Though from the start, basically all of them are tagged with death. Every other sentence describing stuff like how they ignorantly painted radium onto their faces made me say, out loud, "oh no."

Moore pulls no punches in describing their conditions. I winced as she described every awful thing the radium did to those poor womens' bodies. A good number of the people introduced are dead by the first third.

It's in the last third, though, where Moore really locks TF in, describing one particular group of womens' lawsuit against the company that made them work with radium paint. I can't lie, I cried. Every sentence drips with grief and scorn for the scummy corpos that tried to dodge liability at every step of the way, and I was on the edge of my seat (metaphorically, of course) in anticipation of the verdict.

Absolute cinema, this book. Can't recommend it enough.

the wager

david grann

Yeah, another Grann book. This one was written after COVID, but I didn't know that until it contained a reference to social distancing of all things!

Though the synopsis sets you up for a Lord of the Flies-esque story where everyone becomes nothing more than savages by the end, the actual story that's told comes nowhere close to that. I lowkey found it a bit anticlimactic.

However, the actual history of the events doesn't take away from the writing in the slightest. Grann constructs his characters in such an elegant manner that at a certain point I forgot these were real people that he had to learn about through primary sources and whatnot.

All in all, peak book. Killers of the Flower Moon does still have it beat, though.

on the run

gina & gregg hill

As much as Wiseguy tried to show the downfall of Henry Hill in an unflattering way, it still romanticized the gangster life in some respect. This book shatters whatever is left of that.

Written by his kids, it starts around the end of the Lufthansa heist. Even at the beginning, when they're still in primary school, Gina and Gregg pull no punches in chronicling their father's horrific parenting and abuse, from dealing cocaine in the house to hosting depraved orgies.

After that it shifts to the part of the story Wiseguy never covered, probably because Henry had nothing going from him after the gangster life. This is where the conflicts really start to heat up, especially when Gregg grows up and is able to put up more of a fight.

Reading this felt like watchig a car crash in slow motion. It was horrific, but something about it just made me keep on reading to see if any miracle would happen and change the family for the better. Things did get better, but only at the very end, where- actually, maybe I won't say anything for the sake of spoilers.

Just read it yourself- it's good.

okay books

These are books that got me on the hook throughout, but I can only say I somewhat enjoyed reading them. They certainly aren't a waste of time, but I can't call these peak.

the orchid thief

susan orlean

I read the synopsis thinking it was gonna be about the actual court case, but no, like 80% of it is just the author dicking around Florida and yapping with others about orchids. It's fun yapping, no doubt, but I can't lie- I felt a bit cheated after seeing the conflict the cover flaps advertised resolved in such a simpler manner.

There's not even a proper ending. Maybe there's a reason for that which English teachers could make their kids write a whole essay on, but it kinda left a bad taste in my mouth.

So yeah, not the best book, but it's still fine. I don't really regret reading it.

don quixote

miguel cervantes

After seeing a bunch of fanart of Limbus Company's DQ, I really wanted to like this book. (I read this before knowing of the character I think.)

It's a fine book, but there's just way too many tangents. Certain segments were pretty amusing though! The humor holds up surprisngly well after a couple centuries. I can't say I laughed, but I certainly wasn't bored.

Actually, there was one part where I busted my gut laughing. I'll leave it to y'all to guess which one it was.

The way the book works is that it's kinda-sorta split into segments that are basically independent of each other besides a couple of callbacks. If all you knew was the premise and I flipped you to the random start of a segment, you'd have basically no problem reading on.

journey to the center of the earth

jules verne

Real quick, props to the title for being such a concise description of what goes on. Like yeah, I sure as hell did read a journey to the center of the earth.

I actually had to read an abridged Chinese version of this book in my middle school's Chinese immersion program. In my mind I believed they gutted like 90% of the book, but turns out the original thing is just as short, clocking in at around 250 pages.

It's an ok, self-contained little read. I'd read 20k Leagues next, but I already finished it in middle school and there's nothing I hate more than rereading books (besides being drawn and quartered or whatever).

all the president's men

bob woodward & carl bernstein

The writing style of this book is very unique. It's about Watergate and is written by the people who broke the whole thing. They talk about themselves in the third person, and they only directly quote others- never themselves. So yeah, the journalistic writing is pretty cool and I'd read another one with a similar style.

I'm putting this in OK, though, since despite the book's best efforts at the start to remind me who each of the bajillion names are in the White House, I still forgot and had to look crap up on Wikipedia. The plot also got a bit scattered towards the end, and it lowk kinda lost me. Most likely a skill issue, but this is my book review, not others'.

the spy and the traitor

ben macintyre

Who doesn't love a good spy story? This book follows the life of Oleg Gordievsky, a KGB spy who worked as a double agent for MI6. Though told more like nonfiction than fiction, I still found all the various spy splots intriguing enough to keep my attention.

For some reason, despite the book telling us his real name and giving us a photo of him and his house, the epilogue says that he lives under an assumed name. My brother in Christ, what is the point of the anonymity if some schmuck with a tape recorder is going to come and dox you anyways?

dogwater books

hitchhiker's guide to the galaxy

douglas adams

The only good thing about this book is that you get to see where all the references in The Impossible Quiz and the occassional Reddit comment come from.

This series tries so hard to be funny, and yet, I think I only laughed once throughout the entire 700-odd pages of the book. The main mechanism it uses to try to get a laugh is sheer absurdism, but nearly all of it falls flat. After most of the jokes, I just sat there, like, "ok".

So yeah. Don't waste your time.

catch-22

joseph heller

Imagine reading a mathematical proof, but all the paragraphs are out of order.

I think one of the cover quotes was something like "This is the only war novel that makes any sense." What the hell was that person on?
